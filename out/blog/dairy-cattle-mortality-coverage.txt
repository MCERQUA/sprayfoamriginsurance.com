27:Tf4b,<h1>Why a Standard Farm Policy Won't Pay for Dead Dairy Cattle</h1>
<p>Your herd is the single most valuable asset on your dairy. So why do so many operations discover — only after a loss — that their insurance barely covers a dead cow?</p>
<p>For most dairies, the answer is a quiet gap between what feels like coverage and what actually pays.</p>
<h2>The Hidden Gap in Farm-Owner Policies</h2>
<p>A standard farm-owner (FP) policy is built around <strong>buildings and liability</strong>. It covers your barn, your parlor, your equipment, and your liability to others. What it does <strong>not</strong> meaningfully cover is the death of your animals.</p>
<p>When a milking cow dies — from disease, a calving complication, a stray voltage incident, or a barn fire — the FP policy pays little or nothing. A small " livestock" sublimit, if it exists at all, is a fraction of what the animal was worth. For registered and high-genetic stock, the gap can be thousands of dollars per head.</p>
<h2>What Livestock Mortality Actually Covers</h2>
<p>Livestock mortality is the coverage built specifically to insure the herd. A properly structured policy covers death from:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Disease and illness</strong></li>
<li><strong>Accidents</strong> — calving complications, equipment incidents, stray voltage</li>
<li><strong>Disaster</strong> — barn fire, lightning, windstorm</li>
<li><strong>Theft</strong> and, on some forms, mysterious disappearance of scheduled animals</li>
</ul>
<p>It comes in two shapes:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Named-peril (limited) mortality</strong> pays only for listed causes (fire, lightning, certain accidents).</li>
<li><strong>Full (broad) mortality</strong> covers death from almost any cause, including disease.</li>
</ul>
<p>For valuable milking cows and registered breeding stock, most dairies want <strong>full mortality</strong>.</p>
<h2>The Valuation Problem</h2>
<p>Even with mortality coverage in place, <strong>how your animals are valued</strong> determines whether a claim makes you whole. There are two approaches:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Flat rate per head</strong> — typical for grade cattle, valued at a set market rate.</li>
<li><strong>Individual scheduling</strong> — registered, show, and high-genetic animals valued on their own merit.</li>
</ol>
<p>The difference at claim time is enormous. A flat-rate policy might pay $1,800 for a registered Jersey worth $6,000. Proper individual scheduling ensures the claim pays what the animal was actually worth.</p>
<h2>Real Claim Scenarios</h2>
<p><strong>Barn fire.</strong> A fire kills 14 cows and damages the parlor. The FP policy pays to rebuild the barn. The 14 cows? Without livestock mortality, the herd loss is largely uncovered.</p>
<p><strong>Disease outbreak.</strong> A contagious illness moves through the milking herd. Several valuable animals are lost. A named-peril policy excludes disease — only full mortality covers it.</p>
<p><strong>Stray voltage.</strong> Chronic low-level voltage through the parlor suppresses production and, over time, contributes to animal loss. These losses are nearly impossible to recover without mortality coverage and good records.</p>
<h2>What You Can Do Now</h2>
<p>If you're not sure where your coverage stands, take three steps:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Read your current policy's livestock section.</strong> Look for sublimits and perils covered.</li>
<li><strong>Document your herd.</strong> Animal ID, breed, age, and value — registration papers for registered stock. Good records mean fast, full claim payments.</li>
<li><strong>Get a mortality quote.</strong> It's typically 15 minutes on a call, and it protects the asset that drives your income.</li>
</ol>
